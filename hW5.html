Step 2: Link between Monetary Policy Surprises and Economic Indicators

Category 1: Production and Earnings

  • Metric: Production in Industry (INDPRO)
  • Anticipated Outcome: Positive Correlation (+)
  • Justification: Industrial production is closely tied to the fluctuations in monetary policy, particularly in how it affects credit accessibility and interest rates. A surge in interest rates (target) can lead to a reduction in investment and borrowing, which could either boost or hinder industrial production.

Category 2: Employment Sector

  • Metric: Rate of Unemployment (UNRATE)
  • Anticipated Outcome: Negative Correlation (-)
  • Justification: Changes in monetary policy tend to have a reciprocal relationship with the unemployment rate. An elevation in interest rates (target) might diminish economic activities, potentially resulting in job losses and an uptick in unemployment.

Category 3: Real Estate

  • Metric: Initiations of Housing Construction (HOUST)
  • Anticipated Outcome: Positive Correlation (+)
  • Justification: Variations in monetary policy, especially those affecting mortgage rates, can impact the initiation of housing construction. A decrease in interest rates (target) might boost the demand for housing, potentially leading to an increase in housing construction starts.

Category 4: Consumer Spending, Orders, and Stock Levels

  • Metric: Sales in Retail (RETAILx)
  • Anticipated Outcome: Positive Correlation (+)
  • Justification: Consumer spending in retail typically shows a positive response to a decrease in interest rates (target), as lower borrowing costs may incentivize consumers to spend more, possibly enhancing overall consumption.

Category 5: Finance and Lending

  • Metric: Money Supply M2 (M2SL)
  • Anticipated Outcome: Positive Correlation (+)
  • Justification: A monetary policy that is expansionary in nature, characterized by reduced interest rates (target), can lead to an augmentation in the money supply, potentially resulting in an increase in lending and borrowing activities, and thereby a larger M2.

Category 6: Rates of Interest and Foreign Exchange

  • Metric: Yield of 10-Year Treasury Bonds (GS10)
  • Anticipated Outcome: Negative Correlation (-)
  • Justification: There is an inverse relationship between the 10-Year Treasury Bond yields and alterations in interest rates (target). A diminution in interest rates may lead to a corresponding reduction in the yield of 10-Year Treasury Bonds.

Category 7: Inflation and Pricing

  • Metric: Index of Consumer Prices (CPIAUCSL)
  • Anticipated Outcome: Negative Correlation (-)
  • Justification: A reduction in interest rates (target) can trigger an increase in consumer spending, which may subsequently exert inflationary pressures, potentially causing an upward trend in the Consumer Price Index.

Category 8: Equity Markets

  • Metric: Index S&P 500
  • Anticipated Outcome: Positive Correlation (+)
  • Justification: Equity markets generally exhibit a positive response to a monetary policy that is accommodative, such as a reduction in interest rates (target). Lower interest rates might propel investors towards equities in search of higher returns, which could, in turn, elevate stock prices.

def calculate_abnormal_returns(df, models):
    for ret in ['-1', '0', '+1']:
        df[f'AR_{ret}'] = df[f'ret_{ret}'] - (models[f'ret_{ret}'].params['const'] +
                                              models[f'ret_{ret}'].params['target'] * df['target'] +
                                              models[f'ret_{ret}'].params['path'] * df['path'] +
                                              models[f'ret_{ret}'].params['size'] * df['size'])
    return df

def calculate_cumulative_abnormal_returns(df):
    df['CAR_3_day'] = df[['AR_-1', 'AR_0', 'AR_+1']].sum(axis=1)
    return df

def perform_t_test(df):
    mean_CAR = df['CAR_3_day'].mean()
    std_CAR = df['CAR_3_day'].std()
    n = len(df)
    t_stat = mean_CAR / (std_CAR / (n ** 0.5))
